Active Research Portfolio

The Federal Watch Archive maintains an active portfolio of research projects examining various aspects of government transparency and accountability. Each project employs rigorous analytical methods and focuses on documented evidence rather than speculation.

Major Ongoing Projects

Congressional Intelligence Oversight Effectiveness Study

Status: Data Collection Phase • Started: March 1999 • Expected Completion: June 2000

Project Overview:
Comprehensive analysis of House and Senate intelligence committee oversight mechanisms since the 1975 Church Committee reforms. Examining effectiveness of current accountability structures in providing meaningful oversight of intelligence community operations.

Research Components:

  • Analysis of committee hearing transcripts (1975-1999)
  • Review of oversight reports and recommendations
  • Interviews with former committee staff and members
  • Comparative analysis with oversight in other democracies
  • Assessment of classified vs. public oversight effectiveness

Preliminary Findings:
Initial analysis suggests significant variations in oversight intensity across different administrations and committee leadership. Early evidence indicates structural constraints limit effectiveness of current mechanisms.

Data Sources: Congressional records, GAO reports, academic interviews, declassified briefing materials

Multi-Agency Declassification Policy Analysis

Status: Phase II - Agency Comparison • Started: January 1999 • Expected Completion: March 2000

Project Overview:
Systematic examination of declassification procedures and implementation across major federal agencies. Focus on identifying best practices, procedural inconsistencies, and barriers to effective transparency.

Agencies Under Study:

  • Central Intelligence Agency
  • National Security Agency
  • Department of State
  • Department of Defense
  • Federal Bureau of Investigation

Research Methodology:
Comparative analysis of FOIA processing times, declassification guidelines, and public release procedures. Includes quantitative analysis of processing statistics and qualitative assessment of procedural differences.

Current Focus:
Phase II examining why identical document types receive different treatment across agencies. Preliminary data suggests need for standardized procedures.

Unidentified Aerial Phenomena: Government Response and Accountability

Status: Document Collection Phase • Started: August 1999 • Expected Completion: December 2000

Project Overview:
Systematic analysis of U.S. government response to unidentified aerial phenomena reports, focusing on institutional accountability, classification policies, and inter-agency coordination. This research examines documented government programs rather than unexplained phenomena themselves.

Research Components:

  • Analysis of Air Force Project Blue Book records (1952-1969)
  • Review of congressional oversight of UAP-related programs
  • Examination of contractor relationships in advanced aerospace research
  • Assessment of classification and declassification patterns
  • Inter-agency coordination analysis (DoD, CIA, FAA)

Key Research Areas:
Institutional Response: How federal agencies have managed UAP reports and public inquiries since 1947
Contractor Oversight: Government accountability for classified aerospace research contracts
Classification Policy: Consistency in applying national security exemptions to UAP-related materials

Preliminary Observations:
Significant gaps in oversight of classified aerospace programs. Inconsistent application of FOIA exemptions across similar UAP-related requests suggests need for standardized declassification criteria.

Data Sources: Air Force Historical Research Agency, National Archives, congressional hearing transcripts, GAO reports on aerospace contracting

Projects Under Review

Inspector General Effectiveness Across Federal Agencies

Status: Peer Review • Research Completed: November 1999 • Publication: January 2000

Project Summary:
Cross-agency evaluation of Inspector General offices' impact on federal accountability since the 1978 IG Act. Analysis covers 21 years of IG operations across 12 major federal agencies.

Key Research Questions:

  • How effective are IG offices in identifying and preventing waste, fraud, and abuse?
  • What factors contribute to IG office effectiveness variations?
  • How do IG recommendations translate into actual policy changes?
  • What structural reforms might improve IG effectiveness?

Methodology:
Quantitative analysis of IG report recommendations and implementation rates, supplemented by interviews with current and former IG officials and agency leadership.

Preliminary Conclusions:
Significant variation in IG effectiveness correlates with agency culture, leadership support, and resource allocation. Recommendations include structural reforms to enhance independence.

FOIA Implementation Comparative Study

Status: External Review • Research Completed: October 1999 • Publication: December 1999

Project Summary:
Examination of Freedom of Information Act compliance and processing variations across federal agencies. Analysis includes processing times, exemption usage, and appeal success rates.

Data Analysis:
Comprehensive statistical analysis of FOIA annual reports (1990-1998) from 25 major federal agencies. Includes trend analysis and identification of best practices.

Key Findings:
Substantial disparities in processing times and exemption interpretations across agencies. Some agencies consistently outperform others in transparency metrics, suggesting procedural improvements could be standardized.

Recently Completed Projects

Cold War Intelligence Oversight: Lessons for Contemporary Policy

Status: Published • Completed: September 1999 • Published: Intelligence and National Security, Vol. 14, No. 3

Project Summary:
Historical analysis of intelligence oversight evolution from the Church Committee through the end of the Cold War. Identifies lessons applicable to contemporary oversight challenges.

Major Conclusions:

  • Oversight effectiveness varies significantly with political will and public attention
  • Structural reforms alone insufficient without cultural changes
  • Balance between secrecy needs and accountability requirements remains challenging
  • International models offer insights for U.S. oversight improvement

Impact:
Research cited in three subsequent congressional hearings on intelligence oversight reform. Findings incorporated into ongoing policy discussions.

Federal Whistleblower Protection: Effectiveness and Reform Needs

Status: Published • Completed: June 1999 • Published: Public Administration Review, Vol. 59, No. 4

Project Summary:
Analysis of federal whistleblower protection mechanisms and their effectiveness in encouraging accountability. Includes case study analysis and policy recommendations.

Research Scope:
Examination of Whistleblower Protection Act implementation, Office of Special Counsel effectiveness, and alternative disclosure mechanisms across federal agencies.

Policy Recommendations:
Enhanced protections for intelligence community whistleblowers, improved investigation procedures, and strengthened retaliation prevention measures.

Classified Aerospace Programs: Accountability and Contractor Oversight

Status: Published • Completed: April 1999 • Published: Government Accountability Quarterly, Vol. 12, No. 2

Project Summary:
Analysis of government oversight mechanisms for classified aerospace research and development programs, with focus on contractor accountability and congressional oversight effectiveness.

Research Scope:
Examination of oversight procedures for classified contracts with major aerospace contractors including Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Boeing, and Northrop Grumman. Analysis covers 1970-1998 period.

Key Findings:

  • Significant oversight gaps in Special Access Programs (SAPs)
  • Limited congressional visibility into compartmentalized research projects
  • Inconsistent application of cost accounting standards in classified programs
  • Weak institutional memory due to security compartmentalization

Case Studies Examined:
Analysis included review of historical programs where accountability issues became public: Aurora reconnaissance aircraft allegations, stealth technology development oversight, and advanced propulsion research contracts.

Policy Impact:
Research findings cited in subsequent GAO reports on defense contracting oversight. Recommendations for improved SAP oversight adopted by House Defense Appropriations Subcommittee.

Methodological Note:
All analysis based on unclassified sources including congressional hearings, GAO reports, contractor public filings, and declassified program summaries. No classified information was sought or utilized.
